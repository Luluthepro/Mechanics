A closer analogy is an upturned glass of water. Many of you know a classical demonstration when a glass of water covered with a firm sheet is turned upside down and the sheet is held in place by the atmospheric pressure force, which is equivalent to the weight of 10.3 metres of water. But few ponder why the sheet is needed for that demonstration. The answer is Rayleigh–Taylor instability: when a denser liquid (water) is put on top of a rarer liquid (air), any deviation from a flat boundary will grow over time exponentially, ruining the boundary very quickly. This is popularly known as spilling of liquid. This is why a firm sheet is needed: it does not apply any forces, but prevents the Rayleigh–Taylor instability from developing.
